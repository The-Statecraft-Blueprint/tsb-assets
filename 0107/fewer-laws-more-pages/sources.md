# Sources — Legislation Complexity Table
## "Fewer Laws. More Pages."
### Essay: It Begins With "Why?" (GitHub Issue #107)
### Last updated: May 2026

---

## What This Table Shows

Two metrics across five selected Congresses spanning 1947–2010:
1. Public laws enacted per Congress
2. Average pages per statute (total pages of Statutes at Large divided by number of laws enacted)

The table is designed to address the "omnibus consolidation" counterargument — the claim that fewer laws enacted simply reflects packing more policy into larger bills, not gridlock. The table shows that legislation became simultaneously rarer and more complex, which is not consistent with an efficiency explanation.

## Methodology Notes

- **Selected Congresses** are illustrative, not comprehensive. They were chosen to show the trend across representative time periods. Full data for all Congresses is not available in a single standardized source.
- **Pages per statute** is calculated as total pages of public laws in the Statutes at Large for that Congress divided by number of public laws enacted. This is an average; individual laws varied enormously in length.
- **The omnibus note** in the table is an editorial annotation addressing the most common counterargument to the legislation decline data. It is not a data point.
