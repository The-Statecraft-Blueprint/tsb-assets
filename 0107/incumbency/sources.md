# Sources — House Incumbency Reelection Rate
## "Share of U.S. House Incumbents Reelected, 1970–2024"
### Book: *Why Do You Think the Government Doesn't Work?* — Chapter 2, Observation (incumbency)
### Last updated: June 2026

---

## What This Chart Shows

The percentage of U.S. House of Representatives incumbents who sought reelection and won, by election year, 1970–2024.

This is the reelection rate among those who *chose to run* — it excludes members who retired or chose not to seek reelection. It is the standard measure used in political science for incumbency advantage.

## Methodology Notes

- **Who is counted:** Only incumbents who actively sought reelection. Retirements and members who chose not to run are excluded from both numerator and denominator. This is the standard political-science definition of incumbency advantage.
- **Average:** Across the plotted period (1970–2024, 28 elections), the mean reelection rate is **94.0%**. The dashed reference line and the "94% avg." label reflect this plotted-period average. (Over the longer 1954–present span the figure is in the same 93–94% range.)
- **The floor:** Across the plotted period the rate never falls below **85.4%** (2010). In 25 of 28 elections it stayed at or above 90%.

---

## On the "dips" (what the chart labels)

The chart marks only the three elections in the plotted period where incumbent reelection fell **below 90%** — the genuine troughs:

| Year | Rate | Label | Driver |
|------|------|-------|--------|
| 1974 | 87.7% | Watergate | Post-Watergate backlash |
| 1992 | 88.3% | Redistricting | Post-1990 redistricting, House Bank scandal, record retirements |
| 2010 | 85.4% | Tea Party | 2010 midterm wave |

**Deliberately NOT labeled as "waves":** Several elections often called "waves" in partisan-control terms did **not** produce low incumbent-reelection rates and are therefore not marked on this chart:
- **1980** (90.7%) and **1994** (90.2%) — the 1994 "Republican Revolution" flipped House control but still reelected ~90% of incumbents who ran; the turnover came heavily from open seats, not defeated incumbents.
- **2006** (94.0%) and **2018** (91.3%) — party-control "waves" with reelection rates near or above average.

This distinction is the point: even the most famous "throw-them-out" elections rarely unseat more than ~1 in 7 incumbents. Marking only the true reelection troughs keeps the chart honest and consistent with the prose.

---

## What the chart does NOT show

The chart does not show the rate at which vulnerable incumbents **retire** rather than face a difficult election. Effective incumbency protection is therefore somewhat higher than the reelection rate alone suggests, because some at-risk members exit before Election Day. This is a limitation in the conservative direction (it understates entrenchment), and the chart does not attempt to quantify it.

---

## Note for drafting (not for the published chart)

This metric is **ambiguous on its own** — a high reelection rate could indicate voter satisfaction. In the book it is presented as *half of a paradox*: near-total reelection sits next to near-record-low approval (the approval observation). Neither number alone is damning; the pair is. The chart should describe the data; the interpretation lives in the prose.
