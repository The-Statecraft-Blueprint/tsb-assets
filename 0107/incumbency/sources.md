## What This Chart Shows

The percentage of U.S. House of Representatives incumbents who sought reelection and won, by election year, 1970–2024.

This is the reelection rate among those who *chose to run* — it excludes members who retired or chose not to seek reelection. It is the standard measure used in political science for incumbency advantage.

## Methodology Notes

- **Who is counted:** Only incumbents who actively sought reelection. Retirements and members who chose not to run are excluded from both numerator and denominator. This is the standard political science definition of incumbency advantage.
- **Wave election dips:** The chart shows several notable dips — 1974 (Watergate), 1980 (Reagan), 1992 (redistricting/Ross Perot), 1994 (Republican Revolution), 2006 (Democratic wave), 2010 (Tea Party). These are the moments when national forces briefly overcame the structural incumbency advantage. Even in these "wave" years, the rate rarely falls below 85%.
- **The baseline:** Since 1954, the reelection rate has never fallen below 85% and averages approximately 93–94%. The floor is notably high even in the worst years for incumbents.
- **What the chart does NOT show:** The chart does not show the rate at which incumbents choose to retire rather than face a difficult election. The effective incumbency protection is even higher than the reelection rate suggests, because many vulnerable incumbents retire rather than risk defeat.

---

## Key Annotation Notes for Chart

- **Wave elections labeled:** 1974, 1980, 1994, 2006, 2010 — the elections where national forces produced the largest drops.
- **Average line:** A horizontal reference line at 93% illustrates the long-run average.
- **The floor:** Even the lowest point (2010, 85.4%) would be considered an extraordinary reelection rate in most democracies.
