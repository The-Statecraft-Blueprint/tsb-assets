# Legislative Gridlock, 1947–2015 — Sources

## What This Chart Shows
The share of salient national issues that end each Congress in deadlock — i.e., on the legislative agenda but not enacted into law — from the 80th Congress (1947–48) through the 114th (2015–16). It is an *agenda-relative* measure of legislative output: not how many laws passed, but how much of the nation's actual agenda went unresolved. The value rises from roughly a quarter in the late 1940s to a peak of about three-quarters in 2013–14. The chart shows the per-Congress values (jagged) with a 10-year (5-Congress) centered moving-average trend.

## Methodology
- **Measure:** For each Congress, the percentage of "salient" agenda issues that fail to be enacted. Salient issues are identified from unsigned *New York Times* editorials (an issue counts as salient if it draws a threshold number of editorials in a Congress); deadlock is the share of those issues not resolved by enactment.
- **Trend line:** 5-Congress centered moving average (≈10 years), with a shortened window at the series endpoints. Shown to convey the trend without obscuring the genuine Congress-to-Congress volatility.
- **Figures cited:** start 26.09% (80th); peak 73.68% (113th, 2013–14) — reported in text as "roughly three-quarters," not "75%." The "75%" figure that appears in Binder's public writing is a rounded approximation; the database peak is 73.68%.
- **Confidence:** High for the series values (author's own published data, verified against her spreadsheet). The underlying agenda-identification method (NYT editorials) is the measure's main known limitation — see below.
- **Known limitations:** The agenda is constructed from *New York Times* editorials, which may over-weight contentious, nationalized issues and could inflate the absolute level of measured gridlock. This affects the *level* more than the *trend*; the upward movement over time would require the editorial selection itself to have shifted systematically to be an artifact. The series ends at 2015–16 (the author's data does not extend further).

## On attribution (important)
This chart uses Binder's **measurement** (the gridlock score) only. It does **not** adopt her **explanation** of what drives gridlock (her causal account centers on polarization, which is contested in the literature — see Chiou & Rothenberg). The measure is the uncontested part; the cause is deliberately left open here.
