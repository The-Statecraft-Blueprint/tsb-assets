# Legislative Gridlock, 1947–2022 — Sources

## What This Chart Shows
The share of salient national issues that end each Congress in deadlock — i.e., on the legislative agenda but not enacted into law — from the 80th Congress (1947–48) through the 117th (2021–22). It is an *agenda-relative* measure of legislative output: not how many laws passed, but how much of the nation's actual agenda went unresolved. The value rises from roughly a quarter in the late 1940s to a peak of about three-quarters in 2013–14, then falls to roughly half in 2021–22. The chart shows the per-Congress values (jagged, navy) with a 10-year (5-Congress) centered moving-average trend (oxblood).

## Methodology
- **Measure:** For each Congress, the percentage of "salient" agenda issues that fail to be enacted. Salient issues are identified from unsigned *New York Times* editorials (an issue counts as salient if it draws four or more editorials in a single Congress — Binder's stated threshold); deadlock is the share of those salient issues not resolved by enactment.
- **Series variant used:** Binder's dataset reports five variants at different salience thresholds (grid1–grid5). This chart uses **grid4**, the variant matching her published headline figure (the 113th Congress = 73.68%, which she describes as "almost three-quarters" / "worst Congress ever"). The raw fail-count and agenda denominator behind each point are present in the source spreadsheet.
- **Trend line:** 5-Congress centered moving average (≈10 years), with a shortened window at the series endpoints. Shown to convey the trend without obscuring the genuine Congress-to-Congress volatility.
- **Figures cited:** baseline 26.09% (80th, 1947–48); peak 73.68% (113th, 2013–14), reported in text as "nearly three-quarters," **not** "75%" — the rounded "75%" that appears in some public writing is an approximation; the database peak is 73.68%. Most recent point: 48.15% (117th, 2021–22).
- **Confidence:** High for the series values (author's own published data, verified against the source spreadsheet). The agenda-identification method (NYT editorials) is the measure's main known limitation — see below.
- **Known limitations:** The agenda is constructed from *New York Times* editorials, which may over-weight contentious, nationalized issues and could inflate the absolute *level* of measured gridlock. This affects the *level* more than the *trend*: for the editorial selection to manufacture the upward trend, the selection bias itself would have to have worsened systematically over seventy years. Binder also reports (same chapter) that gridlock on *less*-salient issues runs higher than on salient ones over the postwar period — evidence against the view that the metric merely tracks media-amplified conflict (the "Secret Congress" objection).

## On attribution (important)
This chart uses Binder's **measurement** (the gridlock score) only. It does **not** adopt her **explanation** of what drives gridlock (her causal account centers on polarization, which is contested in the literature — see Chiou & Rothenberg). The measure is the well-grounded part; the cause is deliberately left open here and addressed elsewhere.
