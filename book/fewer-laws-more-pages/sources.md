# Fewer Laws, More Pages — Sources

## What This Table Shows
For nine selected Congresses (1947–2024), the number of public laws enacted and the average pages per law (U.S. *Statutes at Large* basis). Laws enacted fall over the long run while average length rises sharply — legislation has become both rarer and longer. The recent Congresses are shown in full (no smoothing): pages-per-law peaks at the 116th–117th (~24) and the 118th drops back to 17.66, so the rise is not monotonic. The robust long-run claim is that a law today runs roughly **7–10× longer** than in the late 1940s.

## Methodology
- **Page unit:** U.S. *Statutes at Large* pages (not slip-law or enrolled-bill pages), matching Brookings Table 6-4 throughout, so the historical rows are comparable to each other.
- **118th row (asterisked):** Brookings has not yet finalized the 118th, so its pages (4,838) were compiled directly from GPO GovInfo (Vol. 137: PL 118-1–34 = 1,600 pp; Vol. 138: PL 118-35–274, ending p. 3238). This DIY basis was calibrated against the 117th, where the DIY total (8,756) matched Brookings (8,742) within +0.16% — so the method is sound, but the row is flagged because it is not Brookings-sourced and the 118th was an unusually unproductive Congress.
- **Confidence:** High for 80th–117th (Brookings, author-maintained). Moderate-high for the 118th (primary GPO compilation, calibrated, but provisional).

## Two limitations stated on the table
1. **Page unit is not stable across time.** The 1983 shift from hot-metal typesetting to electronic photocomposition (plus GPO Style Manual compressions in 1984/2000/2008/2016) increased text capacity per page by an estimated 30–40%. A modern page holds more statutory text than a 1947 page. This means raw page growth *understates* the true growth in statutory length — the limitation cuts in the argument's favor.
2. **The rise is not monotonic.** Pages-per-law bounces in the recent Congresses (111th 19.9 → 115th 17.8 → 116th 24.5 → 118th 17.7). The table shows this honestly. The long-run multiple (≈7–10×) is the claim that survives the volatility.

## What this table does and does not establish
- **Does:** refute "fewer laws = leaner, simpler government." Legislation is rarer *and* longer.
- **Does NOT:** refute the "repackaging / Congress just works differently" thesis. Fewer-but-bigger laws are *consistent* with repackaging. That thesis is addressed elsewhere (by an agenda-relative measure of unresolved issues), not by this table.

## On the 274 vs 276 question
The authoritative public-law count for the 118th is **274** (OLRC Classification Tables, PL 118-1 through 118-274). The "276" figure in some secondary databases results from (a) counting two private laws as public and (b) a signing-date sync error for bills signed in early January 2025 after the 119th convened.
